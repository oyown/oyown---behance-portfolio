Seer.gg Case Study

Rebranding • UX & UI design

Before you start: I know that this case study is longer than it should be. I'm trying to get better at writing, so if you have any feedback let me know.

Dark Mode everything.

In late 2019, Arturo - a finance veteran with a double masters degree (!!?) - reached out on Linkedin to redesign Seer.gg - a platform structured like the stock market, where users could trade eSport teams using a platform currency.

I’m into gaming, and by extension into (some) eSports, but finance was a big mystery to me. I was unsure about taking the project on, until Arturo allowed me to design the brand identity as well.

Rather than make this a massive case study going over everything I did, I’d like to show you the process that led me to designing some of the most unique elements of Seer.gg.

Data dilemma

Every good design is rooted in user data, and this time it wasn’t any different. The challenge however was that the users couldn’t have been more different than each other.

There were predominantly 2 types of users:

1) The young eSports fan with low finances but high eSports knowledge.

Young fan, perhaps a student with side income.

2) The person who knows everything finance but little about eSports.

Financial veteran, like a CFO.

Since they were poles apart, I decided to create a fictional third persona - an overlap of both extreme personas.

Arturo agreed that the “middle-persona” could be anyone in the financial industry who’s into games, and might turn out to be the biggest source of traffic.

A balanced set of attributes
My thinking was this: if I could design for this person, I could easily stretch the boundaries to include the other two.

Gamifying finance

A week into the project, I built (very) low fidelity wireframes and decided to test it out with Arturo’s team - some of them gamers, some knew more about finance. Perfect.

  • Plans to create wireframe →
  • I marked clickable items red

The results were surprising, but looking back it should have been expected:

  • People into games didn’t interact with the financial graphs and charts
  • People in finance loved them and wanted more
  • Arturo himself wanted more control over the data, such as seeing the trend of his past investments

It was clear that dumping every bit of imformation we have on a person would 1) be unnecessary and 2) make it more complex

The solution was abstraction.
Choose difficulty level.

Taking a leaf out of my (long past) developer days, I separated the users into three categories, slowly “lifting” the abstraction on financial elements the higher you went.

To take it a step further, and playing along with the illusion of gaming, I decided to design and name these separations as “levels” - the idea being it’d feel like difficulty settings in an actual game.

Breaking laws

Gaming is one of the few remaining areas where it's still okay to break Jacob's Law, and I wasn't going to give that up.

A design with complex data and numericals meant I needed colors and typography to be legible, have high contrast and demand attention when necessary.

I just need an excuse to use Graphik.

With roots in both finance and gaming, Seer.gg would need to stand out when placed beside age-old financial logos as well as against the flashy eSport ones. For this it needed something different, something dynamic.

Something that’d make people do a double-take.

Making of: Seer & GG

Seems weird to talk about building the identity after talking about the website - but it was easier (and I’d argue better) to build an identity now that I knew more about the users I was trying to design for.

In the context of eSports

Data overload

Platforms concerned with finance will always be chock full of data, and Seer.gg was no different.

NEVER do colored texts....unless they actually work

The graphs however weren’t anything like I had ever seen before.

These weren’t the regular bar or pie charts, these were complicated charts that showed the state of the overall market relative to the team you are trying to invest in - sometimes broken down into time segments.

I went through several blogs and resources figuring out everything from how they were created to how they were perceived by the user. My idea was to figure out how best to highlight the graphs, so I can convey the most information possible with just a glance.

  • Depth charts for Demand vs. Supply
  • Candle charts for changes in price

Live play

The trading screen was the most complex piece of the platform. To give you an idea, here's a typical flow:

  • A user would choose from a list of currently live games
  • They would be taken to a live stream of the game (courtesy of Twitch)
  • A section shows the odds of either team winning
  • They would be able to trade themselves or chat with other users who are in the same lobby
  • They’d also be able to assess the market condition by looking at charts and graphs generated by the transactions of other players (“the market”)
  • Slide over to see the better version → →
  • More highlight on what's important

In most cases, a live stream should be the highlight. But in case of Seer.gg, the user is there to trade - it makes more sense to highlight the state of the current “market”.

So I moved the odds banner up top, and put in links so a person could trade as soon as they land on the page. To make sure they are still able to see the odds while trading - sometimes decisions are made in split seconds - I put the form in a popup at the bottom of the screen.

Appears when needed

I put the Live Stream and chat together - a more traditional experience. The chat featured both: a live broadcast and personal chat with anyone who's online.

A live chat with personal messaging

The trading screen is very complex in itself, but users expect a trading screen to be complex: my priority was to make sure that the most important things were highlighted to the user, and it was clear what to do at every step of the way.

Squeezing this all in mobile however, was a different ballgame.

An app that's not an app

Due to restrictions in the US jurisdiction, Seer.gg could not have an app in any of the app stores. So we decided to have an app-like experience within the mobile browser.

Easier said than done.

Firstly, mobile browsers do not offer as much flexibility as a native app would - 1) they are limited by development and 2) browser elements hamper the experience

This was an exceptional circumstance, and required an exceptional solution.

For all screen

To make sure there's no scope for horizontal scroll, I made the tables more vertical. However, to show better data I let the charts & graphs scroll sideways.

Resized graphs and tables

Complex pages such as the obvious trading page were optimised for the smaller screens. To make sure the user was able to see the odds at all times, I put in a little box which showed the score and tapping that would bring up the trading form.

Popping up when you need it

Every other piece of content was resized to make sure they best fit the smaller screen, even the logos.

Designing for mobile turned out to be a more complex than I had imagined, but it was worth it.

Knocking it home

Identity and style guide for developers

Looking back, I think researching with the team creating the persona was the most vital moment of the process - without it this would have been a shot in the dark.

At the time of writing this, I'm working with the developers and Arturo to make sure there are no problems implementing the design.
