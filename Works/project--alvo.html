Alvo required an interface that worked seamlessly in VR and appealed to the already massive user base of first person shooters. An identity was built comprising of familiar elements from the web & traditional games while still taking advantage of the infinite expanse of VR.

Limited by skillset, not by bezels - Research section

With it's first game in VR, Alvo was looking to enter a market still not dominated by the big names. Present games in VR consisted of mosty battle-royale style gameplay with graphics that looked like it was out of the 90s. While VR is a challenging area to work for, Alvo still wanted to push the edge and give a present day game feel in your head.

New Territory

To take full advantage of the wider field of view in VR, it was important to have an idea where on the plane it was easiest for the player to interact.

It also came into light that even though players have the freedom to turn anywhere they want to, they almost always will be going facing forward instead of backward.

That turns heads.

Unsurprisingly, VR field of view mimicks real life - player focus remains at the center of the field of view thus allowing anything to be placed within a comfortable viewing area.

Overall Interface

The interface was kept minimal down to the very core, only attracting the attention of the player when necessary and taking care to stay out of the way of the gameplay experience.

Turning heads, literally.

Taking advantage of VR over static screens was crucial to the gameplay experience - hence, players are prompted to turn heads to access in-game elements such as leaderboards, stats etc.

However, care was taken so that everything crucial to their success showed up front and center.

Players are also encouraged to turn their heads around - creating a surreal experience that is not often seen in traditional gameplay.

[image of turning head with an enemy there]

Spoilt for choice.

Even though this is a new medium, gamers shouldn't have to learn a new way of navigating it.

Taking a cue from existing games and web UI, illustrations & iconography have been used in various places - from showcasing different levels and modes of the game to assisting gamers with navigation menus.

Lastly, true to the value of doing something different, 3d imagery was used for close-ups within a non-gaming environment.

Level up your game

Gamers won't be gamers if they didn't want to see their progress.

Alvo wouldn't be Alvo if they didn't reward them for it.

With help from thenounproject, military icons were rewared to players in recognition for their skill levels in the game - it's a mark that other players see in-game and in leaderboards.

insert image of military ranks

Designed for personality.

Games are fun, and players love to showcase individuality while having fun. Alvo didn't want them to miss out on that.

Several random pattern & color masks were created to give the players a choice in how they are viewed by their teammates, and how they show up in leaderboards.

Something about the marketplace

Alvo is all about clarity yet have the audacity to stand out from others.

It needed a wordmark that reflected all that, and yet was simple enough to stand out on a crowded online marketplace.

A sans-serif military style typeface mixed with a grunge pattern was just the right mixture, providing the perfect combination of clarity and ruggedness that is often associated with FPS games.

insert image of marketplace

Caring for the gamer.

VR headsets have been said to create a feeling of nausea in certain cases - care has been taken not to include any flashing graphics or distracting animations that might accelerate the feeling.

Addtionally, rounds have been kept at a max limit of 10 mins and a virtual warning has been included if the player is using the headset for a long time.

Lessons learnt

  • A bit of (preferably early) user research can go a long way. It definitely saved us from the calamity of creating a great design for the wrong group of people - a move that probably would have shut the business down.
  • Always break the mold: We came up with ideas to break through in a highly lucrative market, and that was only possible because we analysed and decided to do something different against an oversaturated competition.
  • Knowing code doesn’t hurt. It helped me push a site within just 2 days when things became tough. While that might be a fringe case, tools like Webflow are pushing the bounds and knowing how to develop a website has never been easier. I’m an ex. Frontend dev, and even I use Webflow it’s so good.

I design in Figma so developer handoff is always a breeze, but for now I’m still in touch with the team going over small iterations and ideations over the final design.
